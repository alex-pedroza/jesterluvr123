Land of Milk and Honey

July 31, 2024

Generally...

This book had potential, I think, in its compelling explorations of the dimensions of pleasure. Taste, desire, satisfaction - it's hard to distinguish where food ends and the erotic begins (describing a bite of something as "orgasmic" doesn't seem so scandalous as I once thought). I was intrigued by the ability to weaponize food, dining experiences, and specific sociocultural norms in a game of clever chess - the Eun-Young persona exists entirely in a submissive racialized and sexualized imagination alongside promises of a safe future in which pleasure is not discontinued. That much was clear and fascinating, and I enjoyed the explorations of the narrator's relationship to apetite as checkpoints throughout the story.

But the embellishment fog was frustrating to wade through - the writing works better as small bits of digestible prose rather than a full length novel. Not every sentence had to be cloaked in metaphor. And Aida was a weird character -- I could not really see her motives past her age, even in this post-apocalyptic setting. She was childish in her decision-making, temperamental and unsurprisingly disliked by all the people she worked with. I'm not sure that the reader is supposed to like Aida, even at the end with her sad attempt at atoning for running over that child in her sports car or whatever. She was a more distinguished version of her robot father, always doing impact calculus through her fronts, and cunning in a way that made our narrator attracted to her. I'm not sure there was any more depth than that, of either her or the narrator. And that ending... phew, did not add anything of substance to the story.
